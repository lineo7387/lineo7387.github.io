# Color Theory from a Bauhaus Perspective

The Bauhaus color system was built on a simple truth: primary colors are the most powerful. By understanding how to use red, blue, and yellow effectively, you can create designs with remarkable visual impact.

## The Primary Color System

Bauhaus color theory draws from Itten's color wheel:

### The Three Primaries

- **Red (#D02020)** — Energy, passion, importance
- **Blue (#1040C0)** — Trust, stability, depth
- **Yellow (#F0C020)** — Optimism, clarity, attention

### Color Relationships

- **Complementary**: Colors opposite on the wheel create tension
- **Analogous**: Adjacent colors create harmony
- **Triadic**: Three equidistant colors create vibrancy

## Building a Bauhaus Palette

### Step 1: Establish Your Foundation

Start with a neutral base:

- **Background**: Off-white (#F0F0F0) or near-black (#121212)
- **Text**: High contrast against background

### Step 2: Add Primary Accents

Use primary colors sparingly but boldly:

- Call-to-action buttons
- Important icons and decorations
- Category labels and badges

### Step 3: Integrate Neutrals

Support your primaries with refined neutrals:

- Light gray for subtle backgrounds
- Medium gray for borders and dividers
- Black for text and strong accents

## Color Blocking

Bauhaus design often uses large color blocks:

- Hero sections in solid primary colors
- Cards with colored headers
- Section dividers in accent colors

### Proportions

A classic Bauhaus ratio is 60-30-10:

- **60%**: Dominant neutral color
- **30%**: Secondary color (often primary)
- **10%**: Accent color (highlight)

## Using Color for Meaning

Assign colors semantic meaning:

- **Red**: Warnings, errors, important actions
- **Blue**: Links, information, trust signals
- **Yellow**: Highlights, caution, attention

## Practical Application

### Avoid These Mistakes

1. Too many colors competing for attention
2. Colors without clear purpose
3. Low contrast combinations
4. Saturated colors for large backgrounds

### Best Practices

1. Use neutrals for most of your interface
2. Let primary colors make statements
3. Test contrast ratios for accessibility
4. Consider color blindness in your choices

## The Power of Restraint

Remember: color is most powerful when used sparingly. A single bold accent can convey more than a rainbow of competing hues.
